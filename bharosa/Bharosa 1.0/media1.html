Ex-PayPal exec builds low-cost MF platform

MUMBAI: Sanjay Bhargava, the man credited by PayPal co-founder Elon Musk as being the brains behind the payment company's low-cost account authentication strategy, is setting up a digital 'utility company' to enable micro investments in mutual funds with minimal cost.

See Article

The utility - Bharosa Club - is set up to be a digital platform which will connect even basic bank accounts to all mutual funds through the transfer service companies CAMS and Karvy. To ensure there is zero transaction costs, Bharosa would deploy straight-through processing. Bhargava (58), who has worked on financial inclusion in India, sees Bharosa expanding to a 'buyers' club' where even non-financial businesses will pass on benefits of lower selling costs to the club members.

"Telecom companies and FMCG companies have solved the last mile problem in India. But this has not happened in the financial sector," said Bhargava. "The building blocks are already there in terms of JAM (the universal Jan Dhan accounts, Aadhaar identifiers and Mobile penetration). What we have designed is a paperless platform that will include robot advisory based on a simple algorithm which will enable mutual funds acquire customers without cost," he added.

The idea of using the existing network and using channel partners like business correspondents has led to Bharosa Club, which has been founded by Bhargava, his wife Anita, and Prakash Pai who was formerly with Nucleus Software. Bhargava, a former banker, had helped Citi gain market leadership in its cash management business in India. He was one of the early employees picked by Musk when he founded x.com - the company which metamorphosed into PayPal. In PayPal he found a low-cost workaround for authenticating bank accounts which customers linked to PayPal. Instead of the expensive method of collecting cancelled cheques, the firm simply made a very low value deposit and asked the customer to update the transaction id. Bhargava sees Bharosa as the equivalent of an app store on Android or iPhone. The company does have its own product which it is using to get investors started with mutual funds, but it sees providers eventually offering their own plans. "Our aim is to have an arm of assistants of our own or through channel partners to open folios under a campaign 'Ghar Ghar Mein Folio'," he said.

For collecting monthly subscriptions, Bharosa will use the National Automated Clearing House, which enables banks to debit amounts based on a standing mandate.

Unlike most startups that seek to achieve scale from funding by venture capitalists, Bhargava's aim is to start with Rs 12 crore from a close-knit group which could include a payments bank and an 'impact investor'. Subsequently, he would like this to be an entity that is owned largely by banks. The target is to get a million bank account holders to open a folio with a small investment.

Bhargava's objective is to enable wealth creation among low-income groups through systematic investment in equities without losing any money toward commission. "The idea is to have zero customer acquisition cost until the assets under management touch Rs 5,000 crore."
